Theoretical Foundations

Management focus

A failed acquisition, read through three classic lenses.

Students analyse the build‑vs‑buy decision through Christensen's disruption framework, the Technology Acceptance Model, and Barney's Resource‑Based View — locating the failure not in the algorithm but in strategic posture.

3 Frameworks
this session
01

Disruptive Innovation

Bower & Christensen (1995). Harvard Business Review, 73(1), 43–53.

Why incumbents like IBM misread Watson's clinical promise — disruptive AI capabilities are easily distorted by anchoring to existing customer expectations and margin structures.

02

Technology Acceptance Model

Davis (1989). MIS Quarterly, 13(3), 319–340.

Locates Watson's adoption failure in clinician workflow, not algorithmic capability — perceived usefulness and ease of use as decisive variables.

03

Resource‑Based View

Barney (1991). Journal of Management, 17(1), 99–120.

Underpins the build‑vs‑buy question: should AI capability be cultivated as a rare and inimitable resource, or sourced from vendors with no sustainable advantage?

01

Porter's Value Chain

Porter & Millar (1985). Harvard Business Review, 63(4), 149–160.

A structural lens for mapping where AI enters diagnosis, treatment, billing and patient engagement — and where margin capture is most likely.

Institutional Theory

DiMaggio & Powell (1983). American Sociological Review, 48(2), 147–160.

Theoretical Foundations

Management focus

From sensing change to monetising the asymmetry it creates.

Three economic lenses combine: dynamic capabilities for renewal, two‑sided market theory for monetisation, and the lemons problem to make sense of opacity in AI training data and model provenance.

$15.7T AI contribution

3 Frameworks
this session
01

Dynamic Capabilities

Teece, Pisano & Shuen (1997). Strategic Management Journal, 18(7), 509–533.

Frames data‑and‑AI strategy as sensing, seizing and reconfiguring — testing whether organisations can renew data assets, not merely accumulate them.

02

Platform Economics (Two‑Sided Markets)

Rochet & Tirole (2003). JEEA, 1(4), 990–1029.

Explains how AI firms monetise data by orchestrating two‑sided markets where one side's training signal creates value for the other.

03

Information Asymmetry

Akerlof (1970). Quarterly Journal of Economics, 84(3), 488–500.

Maps the lemons problem onto AI markets: transparency and certification become economic — not just ethical — necessities.

Theoretical Foundations

Management focus

Connected infrastructure rarely succeeds inside one firm.

Three frames converge on the IoT problem: ecosystem co‑evolution, the uneven diffusion of connected capabilities, and the lean discipline of asking whether sensors fix waste — or merely automate it.

40B Connected devices
upper bound, 2030
3 Frameworks
this session
01

Ecosystem Theory

Moore (1993). Harvard Business Review, 71(3), 75–86.

Connected AI succeeds in co‑evolving networks of device makers, platform operators, integrators, and end users — not within a single firm's boundary.

02

Diffusion of Innovations

Rogers (2003). Diffusion of innovations (5th ed.). Free Press.

Explains why early‑adopter sites — smart factories, connected hospitals — move years ahead of laggards, with consequences for capital‑allocation timing.

03

Lean Operations

Womack, Jones & Roos (1990). The machine that changed the world. Rawson Associates.

The benchmark against which AI‑IoT efficiency gains are measured: do sensors genuinely reduce waste, or merely automate an already‑flawed process?

3 Frameworks
this session
01

Stakeholder Theory

Freeman (1984). Strategic management: A stakeholder approach. Pitman.

The foundational tool for AI deployments in welfare, education and justice — where success depends on legitimacy across citizens, regulators, frontline workers and affected communities.

02

Appropriate Technology

Schumacher (1973). Small is beautiful: Economics as if people mattered. Blond & Briggs.

Disciplines the assumption that AI is always the answer — asking when a lower‑cost, community‑centred intervention better serves the people it is meant to help.

03

Triple Bottom Line

Elkington (1997). Cannibals with forks: The triple bottom line of 21st century business. Capstone.

Extends performance measurement of AI initiatives beyond financial return to social and environmental outcomes — language for ESG and impact reporting.

3 Frameworks
this session
01

Business Model Canvas

Osterwalder & Pigneur (2010). Business model generation: A handbook for visionaries, game changers, and challengers. John Wiley & Sons.

The structural backbone of the capstone — a shared template to design and stress‑test AI‑enabled silver‑economy ventures across customers, channels, revenue and partnerships.

02

Blue Ocean Strategy

Kim & Mauborgne (2004). Harvard Business Review, 82(10), 76–85.

Directs students toward under‑served segments of the longevity economy, where value innovation opens new market space outside the crowded medical‑device niche.

Base of the Pyramid

Prahalad (2004). The fortune at the bottom of the pyramid: Eradicating poverty through profits. Wharton School Publishing.

A decisive reframe: older adults as informed consumers whose aggregate purchasing power supports market‑based innovation, not welfare‑defaulted design.
