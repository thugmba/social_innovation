## Course Overview

This graduate-level MBA course examines the intersection of artificial intelligence, management strategy, and social innovation. Rather than focusing on technical implementation, the course approaches AI as a strategic and organisational phenomenon - analysing adoption decisions, value creation, governance, and social consequence through established management frameworks.

Students will engage with seminal theoretical texts alongside contemporary case materials, developing the analytical vocabulary to evaluate AI-driven change across healthcare, data markets, infrastructure, public policy, and the emerging silver economy.

**Format:** 6 weekly sessions · 3 hours each · 18 contact hours total

---

## Week 1 - AI Strategy in Healthcare

### Keywords

AI adoption · Strategic investment · Build vs buy · Vendor risk · ROI framing · IBM Watson lesson

### Management Focus

This session introduces AI adoption as a strategic decision rather than a technical one. The IBM Watson Health case serves as the primary vehicle: despite an initial acquisition investment exceeding $4 billion (including Truven and Merge Healthcare) and partnerships with over 200 hospitals globally, the unit was sold in 2022 for approximately $1 billion - a 75% loss in acquisition value alone. Students will analyse the "build vs buy" decision framework through the lens of failed pilots like MD Anderson’s $62 million oncology project, evaluating the risks of strategic lock-in and how to frame AI investment cases for boards and executive committees.

### Theories & Seminal Readings

**Disruptive Innovation**

> Bower, J. L., & Christensen, C. M. (1995). Disruptive technologies: Catching the wave. *Harvard Business Review*, 73(1), 43–53. [https://hbr.org/1995/01/disruptive-technologies-catching-the-wave](https://hbr.org/1995/01/disruptive-technologies-catching-the-wave)

*Christensen's framework illuminates why established healthcare incumbents like IBM struggled to translate Watson's technological promise into clinical adoption, revealing how disruptive AI capabilities can be misread by firms anchored to existing customer expectations and margin structures.*

**Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)**

> Davis, F. D. (1989). Perceived usefulness, perceived ease of use, and user acceptance of information technology. *MIS Quarterly*, 13(3), 319–340. [https://doi.org/10.2307/249008](https://doi.org/10.2307/249008)

*Davis's model provides the analytical vocabulary for understanding why clinicians resisted Watson Health despite its technical sophistication, locating adoption failure not in the algorithm itself but in perceived usefulness and ease of use within real workflows.*

**Resource-Based View (RBV)**

> Barney, J. (1991). Firm resources and sustained competitive advantage. *Journal of Management*, 17(1), 99–120. [https://doi.org/10.1177/014920639101700108](https://doi.org/10.1177/014920639101700108)

*Barney's RBV underpins the build-vs-buy decision at the heart of AI investment, helping students judge whether AI capability should be cultivated as a rare, inimitable internal resource or sourced through vendors without sustainable advantage.*

## Week 2 - AI in the Medical Sector

### Keywords

Market entry · Regulatory strategy · Partnership models · Liability management · Value chain disruption · Innovation pipeline

### Management Focus

This session examines the medical sector as a regulated market where AI deployment requires navigating institutional complexity as much as technological capability. As of mid-2024, the FDA has authorized over 950 AI/ML-enabled medical devices, with radiology accounting for 80% of clearances. Topics include regulatory strategy, approval timelines, and the management of liability in AI-assisted clinical decisions. Students will map an AI-augmented value chain in a global healthcare AI market projected to grow from $20 billion in 2024 to over $600 billion by 2033, assessing where competitive disruption is most likely to occur.

### Theories & Seminal Readings

**Porter's Value Chain**

> Porter, M. E., & Millar, V. E. (1985). How information gives you competitive advantage. *Harvard Business Review*, 63(4), 149–160. [https://hbr.org/1985/07/how-information-gives-you-competitive-advantage](https://hbr.org/1985/07/how-information-gives-you-competitive-advantage)

*Porter's value chain provides the structural lens for mapping where AI inserts itself across diagnosis, treatment, billing, and patient engagement, allowing students to identify the specific links where competitive disruption and margin capture are most likely.*

**Institutional Theory**

> DiMaggio, P. J., & Powell, W. W. (1983). The iron cage revisited: Institutional isomorphism and collective rationality in organizational fields. *American Sociological Review*, 48(2), 147–160. [https://doi.org/10.2307/2095101](https://doi.org/10.2307/2095101)

*DiMaggio and Powell explain why medical AI deployments converge on similar regulatory pathways, certification routines, and partnership templates, offering a sociological account of how institutional pressures shape strategy in highly regulated markets.*

**Transaction Cost Economics (TCE)**

> Williamson, O. E. (1981). The economics of organization: The transaction cost approach. *American Journal of Sociology*, 87(3), 548–577. [https://doi.org/10.1086/227496](https://doi.org/10.1086/227496)

*Williamson's TCE supplies the economic logic behind partnership models between AI vendors and healthcare providers, framing liability allocation and contract design as a question of governance choice under uncertainty and asset specificity.*

## Week 3 - AI & Data as Strategic Assets

### Keywords

Data governance · Competitive moat · Platform strategy · Monetisation · Risk & compliance · Organisational readiness

### Management Focus

Data is increasingly treated as a strategic asset, with AI projected to contribute up to $15.7 trillion to the global economy by 2030 through productivity gains and data-driven consumption. This session examines how AI amplifies the value of proprietary data - and the managerial decisions required to capture that value. Topics include data governance frameworks, the economics of platform competition, and the problem of information asymmetry in AI-driven markets (e.g., patient data, consumer profiling, algorithmic pricing). Students will also assess organisational readiness for AI-led data strategies in a market where 60% of potential value remains trapped in ungoverned data silos.

### Theories & Seminal Readings

**Dynamic Capabilities**

> Teece, D. J., Pisano, G., & Shuen, A. (1997). Dynamic capabilities and strategic management. *Strategic Management Journal*, 18(7), 509–533. [https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1097-0266(199708)18:7%3C509::AID-SMJ882%3E3.0.CO;2-Z](https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1097-0266(199708)18:7%3C509::AID-SMJ882%3E3.0.CO;2-Z)

*Teece and colleagues frame data-and-AI strategy as a question of sensing, seizing, and reconfiguring, helping students assess whether an organisation can continually renew its data assets rather than merely accumulate them.*

**Platform Economics (Two-Sided Markets)**

> Rochet, J.-C., & Tirole, J. (2003). Platform competition in two-sided markets. *Journal of the European Economic Association*, 1(4), 990–1029. [https://doi.org/10.1162/154247603322493212](https://doi.org/10.1162/154247603322493212)

*Rochet and Tirole's platform economics explain how AI-driven firms monetise data by orchestrating two-sided markets, offering pricing and governance logic for businesses where users on one side generate the training signal that creates value for the other.*

**Information Asymmetry**

> Akerlof, G. A. (1970). The market for "lemons": Quality uncertainty and the market mechanism. *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 84(3), 488–500. [https://doi.org/10.2307/1879431](https://doi.org/10.2307/1879431)

*Akerlof's lemons problem maps directly onto AI markets where buyers cannot easily verify the quality, provenance, or bias of training data and models, framing transparency and certification as economic - not just ethical - necessities.*

## Week 4 - AI & IoT Operations

### Keywords

Operational efficiency · Smart infrastructure · Ecosystem management · Scalability · Capital allocation · Supply chain AI

### Management Focus

The Internet of Things creates a new operational layer for organisations - one that is expected to connect 30 to 40 billion devices by 2030. This session examines AI-IoT integration through an operational and ecosystem lens: how do firms allocate capital across connected infrastructure in a market whose potential economic value is estimated between $5.5 trillion and $12.6 trillion? Students will investigate how the diffusion of IoT capabilities - which could add $14 trillion to the global economy via industrial efficiency - shifts competitive dynamics in smart manufacturing, connected health, and urban infrastructure.

### Theories & Seminal Readings

**Ecosystem Theory**

> Moore, J. F. (1993). Predators and prey: A new ecology of competition. *Harvard Business Review*, 71(3), 75–86. [https://hbr.org/1993/05/predators-and-prey-a-new-ecology-of-competition](https://hbr.org/1993/05/predators-and-prey-a-new-ecology-of-competition)

*Moore's ecosystem framing fits the IoT context precisely, since AI-enabled connected infrastructure rarely succeeds inside a single firm's boundary and instead depends on co-evolving networks of device makers, platform operators, integrators, and end users.*

**Diffusion of Innovations**

> Rogers, E. M. (2003). *Diffusion of innovations* (5th ed.). Free Press.

*Rogers's diffusion model helps students understand the uneven uptake of IoT capabilities across industries, explaining why early-adopter sites (smart factories, connected hospitals) move years ahead of laggards and what this implies for capital allocation timing.*

**Lean Operations**

> Womack, J. P., Jones, D. T., & Roos, D. (1990). *The machine that changed the world*. Rawson Associates.

*Womack, Jones, and Roos provide the operational benchmark against which AI-IoT efficiency gains are measured, prompting students to ask whether sensor-driven optimisation genuinely reduces waste or merely automates an already-flawed process.*

## Week 5 - AI for Social Impact

### Keywords

Stakeholder mapping · Public-private partnership · ESG integration · Social ROI · Appropriate technology · Change management · Inequality

### Management Focus

This session shifts the unit of analysis from the firm to society. Public sector AI spending is projected to reach $85 billion by 2030, with nations like Saudi Arabia dedicating $40 billion infrastructure funds to AI-led transformation. Students will examine how AI is deployed in welfare, education, and justice systems - and the management challenges of accountability and bias. A central tension runs through the session: when is high-tech the right answer, and when does it displace lower-cost solutions? Students will apply stakeholder analysis and social return on investment (SROI) frameworks to cases where AI-driven risk mitigation can reduce unintended societal consequences by up to 40%.

### Theories & Seminal Readings

**Stakeholder Theory**

> Freeman, R. E. (1984). *Strategic management: A stakeholder approach*. Pitman.

*Freeman's stakeholder model is the foundational tool for analysing AI deployments in welfare, education, and justice systems, where success depends on legitimacy across citizens, regulators, frontline workers, and affected communities - not only on shareholders or procurers.*

**Appropriate Technology**

> Schumacher, E. F. (1973). *Small is beautiful: Economics as if people mattered*. Blond & Briggs.

*Schumacher's appropriate-technology thesis disciplines the assumption that AI is always the right answer, asking students to consider when a lower-cost, community-centred intervention better serves the people it is meant to help.*

**Triple Bottom Line**

> Elkington, J. (1997). *Cannibals with forks: The triple bottom line of 21st century business*. Capstone.

*Elkington's triple-bottom-line frame extends performance measurement of AI initiatives beyond financial return to include social and environmental outcomes, supplying the language needed to integrate AI investments into ESG and impact reporting.*

## Week 6 - Smart Silver Economy Business Model

### Keywords

Market sizing · Business model canvas · Value proposition · Customer segmentation · Revenue streams · Social enterprise · Longevity economy

### Management Focus

The final session integrates the course's themes into a capstone business model exercise. The global longevity economy is projected to reach $27 trillion by 2030, with the 50-plus cohort expected to account for 54% of all global consumption ($52 trillion). Despite this, most organisations default to welfare-framing rather than market-framing. Students will apply the Business Model Canvas to design AI-enabled products for older adults, evaluate blue ocean opportunities in a segment that controls 34% of global wealth, and stress-test their models against Prahalad's Base of the Pyramid logic. Session concludes with student project presentations.

### Theories & Seminal Readings

**Business Model Canvas**

> Osterwalder, A., & Pigneur, Y. (2010). *Business model generation: A handbook for visionaries, game changers, and challengers*. John Wiley & Sons.

*Osterwalder and Pigneur's canvas is the structural backbone of the capstone exercise, giving students a shared template to design and stress-test AI-enabled silver-economy ventures across customers, channels, revenue streams, and partnerships.*

**Blue Ocean Strategy**

> Kim, W. C., & Mauborgne, R. (2004). Blue ocean strategy. *Harvard Business Review*, 82(10), 76–85. [https://hbr.org/2004/10/blue-ocean-strategy](https://hbr.org/2004/10/blue-ocean-strategy)

*Kim and Mauborgne's framework directs students toward the under-served segments of the longevity economy, where value innovation can open new market space rather than competing inside the crowded medical-device or eldercare niches.*

**Base of the Pyramid (BoP)**

> Prahalad, C. K. (2004). *The fortune at the bottom of the pyramid: Eradicating poverty through profits*. Wharton School Publishing.

*Prahalad's BoP logic offers the decisive reframe of the silver economy, prompting students to treat older adults as informed, value-conscious consumers whose aggregate purchasing power supports genuine market-based innovation rather than welfare-defaulted design.*
